## Abstract

Recent literature has argued that decentralization can create new forms of leverage for criminal gangs, affecting their territorial presence and levels of violence. This paper reverses the causal arrow and analyzes how gangs affect the performance of decentralized institutions. In particular, we study the case of El Salvador, a country with ubiquitous presence of gangs, and find that higher presence of gangs reduced municipalities' fiscal revenues, therefore increasing their dependence on transfers from the central government. This result is mostly found in mid-size municipalities, as the effect is not significant for small and large municipalities. In addition to depressing revenues, gang presence was also associated with lower municipal spending, particularly in the case of capital spending and in small municipalities, and less service provision. We also find that gangs undermine economic activity, not just in the municipalities where they operate but also through spillover effects in neighboring jurisdictions.

**Keywords:** gangs, decentralization, municipal finance, El Salvador

## 1. Introduction

Can decentralization promote local development in countries with a significant presence of non-state armed actors? Scholars have engaged in a robust debate on this topic. According to the more-optimistic strand of the literature, decentralization can help reduce violence, as it helps address some of the grievances that led non-state actors to initiate their violence in the first place.

For instance, decentralization can facilitate the tailoring of policies to fit the particular ethnic and/or cultural practices of subnational regions (Bland, 2006;

Brinkheroff, 2011). Decentralization can also give rebel organizations the opportunity to transform themselves into political parties, win local elections, and take control of subnational governments that now have real resources and responsibilities (Bermeo, 2002; Stepan, 2001). Decentralization can even reduce the stakes associated with control of the central state, reducing incentives to rebel.

Another strand of literature is less optimistic, arguing that decentralization can lead to increased violence, hurting local development. There are several reasons this might occur. For instance, in decentralized environments, the different levels of government often fail to act cohesively as a single decisionmaking body, enabling criminal organizations to grow and increase levels of violence (Durán-Martínez, 2018; Rios, 2013). Another reason decentralization can hurt regional development is that it can exacerbate conflicts if it empowers regionally oriented political parties that foster secessionist sentiment (Brancati, 2006). Decentralization can also allow rebel groups to penetrate subnational governments and use decentralized resources to engage in service delivery, thereby enhancing their legitimacy as they continue to try to overthrow the central state (Eaton, 2006).

In this paper, we propose a novel argument for how decentralization and gang violence might be interrelated, hurting local development. We argue that gangs can undermine the ability of decentralized institutions to function as intended, hurting state capacities, and therefore economic development. In particular, the territorial presence of gangs can limit local fiscal autonomy because the rents they extort from local populations have a depressing effect on the ability of municipalities to collect their own revenues, as citizens who are

extorted by gangs had fewer resources to comply with their local fiscal obligations. In addition to depressing tax revenues, the presence of gangs can reduce municipal spending and limit service provision.

We test the argument presented above in El Salvador. El Salvador is an interesting case study as decentralization played a key role in helping the country overcome the legacies of its deadly civil war, a twelve-year conflict (1980–1992) during which armed rebel organization unsuccessfully sought to overthrow a national government dominated by a narrow set of economic elites. While these elites continued to control the national government in the years that followed the conflict through the electoral success of the Alianza

Republicana Nacionalista (ARENA) party, the rebels transformed their organization into a political party that was able to win elections in a growing number of municipalities, including eventually in the capital city of San

Salvador, and the national government (Holland, 2016). Electoral rules regarding municipalities that guaranteed a majority for the mayor’s party meant that those municipalities under the control of the Frente Farabundo

Martí para la Liberación Nacional (FLMN) party became meaningful spaces of political opposition vis-à-vis ARENA at the national level.

Meanwhile, many local governments experimented with the use of innovative participatory mechanisms to broaden decision making beyond partisan politicians (Bland, 2011), and fiscal decentralization in the form of moregenerous revenue sharing gave all municipal governments a greater share of fiscal revenues. The transformation of local governments into critical spaces of political contestation laid the foundations for the FMLN’s eventual presidential victories in 2009 and 2014 along with the practice of ideologies alternating in power. El Salvador’s relatively stable two-party system has since been transformed by the rise of Nayib Bukele’s newly hegemonic Nuevas Ideas (New

Ideas) party, but decentralization nevertheless deserves much of the credit for the political advances that El Salvador made in its emergence from internal armed conflict, namely the transformation of a rebel group into a competitive political party.

While decentralization helped a non-state armed actor transform itself into a political party that eventually won the presidency, El Salvador’s experience with decentralized governance was subsequently undermined by a non-state armed actor of a very different sort: criminal gangs. Gangs (or maras)

date back to the 1960s in El Salvador, but the problem significantly worsened when the United States returned approximately 40,000 convicted criminals to

El Salvador after the end of the civil war, including Salvadorans who had joined either the Mara Salvatrucha (MS-13) or Barrio 18 in Los Angeles (Sviatschi, 2022).1

Over time, and partially in response to the adoption of hard-line (mano dura) policies by both ARENA and FMLN governments, gangs have transformed from

“loose neighborhood-level federations that operated largely autonomously and lacked internal governing bureaucracies” into “hierarchically organized structures with organizational bases inside the country’s prisons” (Moncada, 2021, 91). With 60,000 members operating in El Salvador, gangs have operated in almost all of the 262 municipalities, where weaknesses in the rule of law enabled them to engage in a wide range of extortion activities. In particular, criminal taxation became a “regular, costly, and humiliating facet of everyday life” (Moncada, 2021, 96).

This report shows that gangs negatively impacted the ability of decentralization to deliver on its promise in El Salvador, with several effects standing out. In one in particular, related to revenue collections, we find that territorial presence of gangs limited the capacity of municipalities to collect their own revenues. This might be related to citizens having lower incomes and abilities to pay as a consequence of the rents gangs extracted from them. This negative effect of gangs on municipal own-revenue collections is seen in midsize municipalities, as no statistically significant effect is found for small and large municipalities.

In addition to these negative fiscal outcomes, we find that the presence of gangs was associated with lower municipal spending and limited service provision. The drop in municipal spending was particularly strong in capital spending and in small municipalities. Along with these negative fiscal outcomes, we find that gangs undermined economic activity, not just in the municipalities where they operate but through spillover effects in neighboring jurisdictions as well. This might be related to gangs inhibiting factor mobility between municipal jurisdictions, through activities such as limiting labor flows

## 2. Decentralization and Non-State Armed Actors

In many countries of the Global South, the state does not enjoy a monopoly on the legitimate use of force, and instead its attempt to control the national territory is contested by a range of challengers who themselves are able to exert territorial control (Luna and Giraudy, 2017). Scholars have engaged in a robust debate on the merits of decentralization in these contexts. According to the more-optimistic arguments in the literature, decentralization can address some of the grievances that have led non-state actors to initiate rebellion in the first place, including a lack of responsiveness on the part of national governments in faraway capitals, and the inability of subnational governments to tailor policies to fit the particular ethnic and/or cultural practices of subnational regions (Bland, 2006; Brinkheroff, 2011). Decentralization can generate a number of other positive effects in these environments by lowering the stakes associated with control of the central state and by giving rebel organizations the opportunity to transform themselves into political parties able to win power over subnational governments that now have real resources and responsibilities (Bermeo, 2002; Stepan, 2001).

Other scholars have been more cautionary and argue that decentralization can backfire.

According to

Dawn

Brancati (2006), decentralization is likely to exacerbate conflicts if it empowers regionally oriented political parties that stand to gain electorally by further fanning the flames of grievances, including secessionist sentiment. In other cases, rebel groups can penetrate subnational governments and use decentralized resources to engage in service delivery, thereby enhancing their legitimacy as they continue to try to overthrow the central state (Eaton, 2006).

Not all non-state armed actors seek to overthrow the state or secede from it, however. Criminal gangs, in sharp contrast to rebels, insurgents, and guerrillas, pose a different set of challenges. The concern is not that gangs use decentralized resources to strengthen the drive to secede from the country or to bolster their prospects of defeating the government militarily. In contrast, in a less-visible but potentially more-pervasive dynamic, gangs can disrupt the various causal mechanisms that have led theorists to posit a positive relationship between decentralization and improved developmental outcomes.

One of the main arguments for decentralization is that it enables subnational governments to compete with one another through the mechanism of factor mobility (Oates, 1972, 2005; Tiebout, 1961). Labor and capital may more easily flow between neighboring subnational jurisdictions than they can between countries, and voters might be able to “vote with their feet” by opting to relocate to local jurisdictions where policy offerings better match their preferences

(Ter-Minassian, 1997).

Competition between subnational governments for capital investment and human resources can encourage local politicians to adopt the kinds of “market-preserving” policies that enhance the country’s overall prospects for economic growth (Weingast, 1995).

However, through a number of illicit behaviors, gangs can limit factor mobility by extracting payoffs from economic actors seeking to transport goods between municipalities, and by imposing checkpoints that limit the mobility of local residents. Criminal organizations interact with businesses in ways that powerfully affect the local economic environment in which they operate

(Fenizia, 2018), not just in illegal markets but in formal markets as well, where they can affect levels of production, employment, and prices (Ferrante,

Fontana, Reito, 2021; Montoya, 2016; Rozo, 2014). In some cases, scholars have documented salary variations that are due chiefly to restrictions imposed on the mobility of less-skilled labor in localities with a high gang presence; higher labor costs due to these restrictions may result in higher production costs

(Roso, 2014).

Gangs can also disrupt another important mechanism through which decentralization is intended to improve fiscal outcomes, which is the ability of local governments to collect own-source revenues.

When fiscal decentralization takes the form of devolving tax bases to subnational governments (rather than sharing revenues that are generated through centrally collected taxes), it can have a host of positive impacts for accountability, encouraging local taxpayers to demand that their local governments use own-source revenues to provide services (Azfar et al., 1999).

Voters may have an easier time monitoring how local governments use the taxes that the voters pay to those local governments than is the case for taxes owed to the central government. At the same time, the political and administrative costs associated with collecting unpopular local taxes can lead to halfhearted or under-resourced local tax efforts, discouraging local authorities from collecting the tax bases they have been assigned. While insufficient local tax effort deserves the attention it has received from scholars of decentralization, it is important to note that widespread gang activity may also be impeding efforts by local authorities to collect taxes. Impoverished residents may be unable to pay “rent” to the criminal gangs who are directly and violently extorting them, and to pay the municipal taxes and fees they are legally obligated to pay. If both gangs and mayors (along with the rest of each local government) are trying to tap the same resources, there are good reasons to expect that gangs will win out.

When gangs can inhibit the collection of local taxes and fees by local governments, they can trigger a number of other negative fiscal outcomes identified by the literature on decentralization. For example, the inability of local governments to collect local taxes is likely to worsen the government’s dependence on fiscal transfers from the center (Rodden, Eskeland, Litvack, 2003). A large body of literature has emphasized the perverse incentives that transfer systems can generate, especially when they take the form of nonearmarked revenues that local governments consider to be “blank checks”

(Bahl and Martinez-Vazquez, 2006; Inman and Rubinfeld, 1997). Transfer systems can also increase the scope for politically oriented discretion on the part of the national government. In addition to worsening the reliance on transfers, the inability to collect local revenues because criminal gangs are tapping the same resource base may lead to increased levels of local debt and pressure for bailouts (Di Cataldo and Mastrorocco, 2021; Rodden et al., 2003).

If criminal gangs can obstruct local tax collection and increase the reliance on transfers, they can also powerfully affect the relationship between decentralization and service provision by local governments. Here scholars have pointed to two problematic dynamics. The first is that the presence of gangs may make it more difficult for neighboring municipalities to use rival approaches to service provision as a means of differentiating themselves from one another (as anticipated by theories of fiscal federalism).

The second is that gangs can also inhibit the mobility of factors of production between municipal jurisdictions through their capacity to control territory and establish checkpoints that act as toll taxes on the movement of people, public transportation, and goods and services (Brown, Montero, Schmidt-Padilla, and Sviatschi, 2020; Melnikov, Schmidt-Padilla, and Sviatschi, 2021). Di Cataldo and Mastrorocco (2020) argue that municipalities where organized crime has taken hold experience a distortion in the provision of public services due to a variety of rent-seeking activities. Bueno (2022) and

Córdova (2017) likewise attribute significant impacts on service provision to gang activity. In addition to shaping the mix of service provision, gangs can impact the overall level of service as well. Several scholars have also documented how organized crime reduces the incentives for local governments to provide critical goods and services across the board and their capacity to do so, starting with the protection of citizens (Acemoglu, De Feo, De

Luca, 2020; Arends, 2021; Beraldo et al., 2017; Melnikov et al., 2021; Pinotti, 2015).

Indeed, regarding one especially salient mechanism of local government, a government’s perceived need to provide services to ingratiate itself with local residents may be depressed when the municipality is being held captive by gangs (Beraldo et al., 2017).

## 3. The Evolution of Decentralization, Local Finances, and Gang Violence

This section presents the main features of fiscal decentralization arrangements and some municipal fiscal results in El Salvador. Appendix 1 and 2 summarize the extent of gang violence across Salvadorean municipalities.

### 3.1 The Institutional Structure of Municipal Governance in El Salvador

Reflecting its unitary status and highly centralized past, El Salvador maintained until 2023 just one level of subnational government composed of 262 municipalities, and these vary significantly in population, economic dynamism, and levels of urbanization. At the intermediate level, El Salvador is organized into 14 departments whose function it is to represent the central state and whose authorities (governors) are appointed by the national government rather than elected in their own right. Although decentralization occurred just at the municipal level of government, there were three dimensions of change: political, administrative, and fiscal.2

In the political sphere, the electoral rules for municipal elections allow parties to present closed lists that are topped by the mayoral candidate and followed by as many candidates as there are seats on the municipal council

(Córdova and España-Nájera, 2021). Prior to the 2015 municipal electoral reform, the winning party was guaranteed complete control of the municipal council, with no opposition seats. Since then, opposition parties have been allowed to gain representation on the council, but the mayor’s party is still guaranteed a majority. Despite the highly partisan and majoritarian design associated with municipal governance, political decentralization also led to the emergence of bipartisan bargaining and coordination among mayors within the Corporation of Municipalities of the Republic of El Salvador (COMURES), which sought to defend vis-à-vis the center the roles played by municipalities (Bland, 2011).

2 The municipal code assigns a number of other key roles to municipalities, including trash collection; municipal policing; the construction of housing; the regulation of local transport, commercial activities, and restaurants; and the promotion of industrial development, education, and health (Vargas, 2022).

In addition to providing for elections, the 1980 municipal code in El

Salvador provides for a number of other mechanisms to enhance participation, including local development councils and open assemblies (cabildos abiertos) several times a year. As Bland argues, “local government allowed for alternation in executive power and demonstrated that turnover of government from one party to another was possible without inciting violence” (Bland, 2011, 866).

El Salvador is unusual in Latin America in that it denies municipalities the ability to levy property taxes.3 Instead municipal governments can collect direct fee-based taxes paid by individuals and companies that develop economic activities within the district. Municipalities can also raise non-tax revenues from user fees and charges, as well as revenue from the sale of goods and services. In addition to those own-source revenues, municipalities received transfers through a revenue-sharing system that was developed beginning in

1988 in the form of the Fund for Municipal Economic and Social Development

(Fondo para el Desarrollo Económico y Social or FODES). FODES funds are distributed according to population, equity, poverty, and land area criteria, and the municipalities that receive the funds are required to devote 80 percent of the revenues to capital investment projects and no more than 20 percent to operating expenses.

During the period 2010–2018, two aspects characterized the municipal finances in El Salvador: (i) high reliance on intergovernmental transfers, and (ii) low generation of own-source revenues. At an aggregate level, FODES transfers represented 48 percent of total municipal revenues during the analyzed period.

258 municipalities in El Salvador for the years 2011–2018. We analyze the effects on fiscal outcomes as well as on local economic activity.

### 4.4 Impacts

**Effects on fiscal management**

The results suggest that small-sized localities are particularly affected by the presence of the gangs, leading to significant reductions of expenditure. Further research is necessary to explore the underlying factors contributing to these varying effects across different population sizes.

## 5. Conclusions

This paper provides empirical evidence that fiscal results of municipal governments are negatively impacted when gangs control territory. In El

Salvador, the territorial control of gangs is associated with lower municipal tax revenues, lower municipal expenditures, and decreased municipal capacity to provide public services. We found a high sensitivity in revenue collection.

According to the results, for each increase by 1 in homicides by gangs per 1,000 inhabitants leads to a reduction in revenue per capita by US$1.1. This statistically significant result is mostly found in medium-sized municipalities, which, for this study, were those with 10,000 to 50,000 inhabitants. We found that in municipalities of this size, the impact of gang activities is US$5.6 for each homicide attributed to gangs per 1,000 inhabitants. In municipalities of other sizes, the effect is not statistically significant.

The presence of MS-13 also has a significant and negative impact on municipal spending per capita. Specifically, on average, for each 1 percent increase in homicides by gangs, the per capita expenditure decreases by

US$17.9. Here, the greatest impact is on the smallest municipalities, where the drop in expenditures per capita is US$20 per 1 homicide per 1,000 inhabitants.

When disaggregating current and capital expenditures, we found that for every extra homicide per 1,000 inhabitants, capital expenditures drop by US$19.7, while current expenditures drop US$2.9. In terms of service provision, there is a significant and negative correlation between the presence of gangs and public service provision, particularly in medium-size municipalities, where garbage collection decreased by 0.2 percent per 1,000 inhabitants due to gang presence.

We also found a significant negative effect on local economic activity and spillover effects to neighboring localities. Results indicate that for every homicide attributed to gangs in general that occurs in one locality (see

Appendix 8), the consumption of electricity within the local territory is reduced by 3.1 million kWh and consumption in neighboring localities is reduced by 5.2 million kWh. Considering that the average consumption per municipality in a year is 29 million kWh, this reduction represents a reduction of almost 10 percent on local economic activity.

Our research revealed that, in addition to the adverse impacts on ownsource revenues, gangs also exerted inhibitory effects on the level of local

expenditure within affected municipalities. This phenomenon can be attributed to the gangs’ ability to control territorial areas, allowing them to impose toll taxes on the movement of factors, including people, public transportation, and goods and services between municipal jurisdictions, a result that was also reported in Brown, Montero, Schmidt-Padilla, and Sviatschi (2020). Consequently, the local governments’ capacity to spend on essential services and infrastructure is constricted, as their ability to collect taxes and generate income is hampered by gang-imposed toll taxes. The disparities observed among municipalities of different sizes necessitate further investigation into the intricate interplay between economic factors, administrative capacity, and territorial characteristics that influence municipal sensitivity to gang-related issues.

Finally, we find that gang activities had a negative impact not just on the municipality in which they operated but also in adjacent and neighboring municipalities. Understanding these dynamics is crucial for devising effective strategies to address the financial challenges posed by gangs and restore the economic growth of affected municipalities.
